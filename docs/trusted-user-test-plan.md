# Trusted-User Test Plan

**Project:** Before We Build  
**Path:** Start Alone / personal preparation before serious relationship decisions  
**Artifact type:** small trusted-user validation plan  
**Status:** Draft plan for 5–10 trusted users  
**Created:** 2026-05-13  
**Inputs:** `docs/generated-report-review-rubric.md`, `wiki/concepts/report-caveat-standard.md`, `docs/personal-preparation-map-template.md`, `docs/start-alone-question-library-v0.md`, `docs/start-alone-question-library-spiritual-safety-review.md`

## 1. Purpose

This plan tests whether the first Start Alone experience helps trusted Christian users slow down, understand the output, notice what is clear and unclear, and prepare for wiser conversation before a serious relationship decision.

This is not a test of compatibility prediction. It does not ask whether the tool can identify a spouse, rank a match, predict marriage outcomes, measure readiness, or discern God’s will. It tests clarity, spiritual safety, usefulness, caveat understanding, trust without over-trust, and missing topics.

Primary research question:

```text
Does the Start Alone question flow and personal preparation map help a trusted user have clearer, more honest, more humble preparation for wise conversation, without treating the tool as a verdict or spiritual authority?
```

## 2. Non-goals and hard boundaries

Do not test or report any of the following:

- compatibility prediction;
- spouse, family, marriage, calling, or match likelihood;
- readiness scores;
- typology accuracy;
- AI simulation quality;
- whether the system knows hidden motives;
- whether the output gives the “right decision.”

If a participant tries to use the test that way, record it as an over-trust risk or expectation mismatch, then redirect:

```text
For this test, we are not checking whether Before We Build can choose or predict a relationship. We are only checking whether the questions and report help you understand what should be brought to Scripture, prayer, church, wise counsel, and honest conversation.
```

## 3. Participants

Target sample: 5–10 trusted users.

Recommended mix:

- 3–5 single Christian brothers/sisters who are close enough to the target audience to judge usefulness;
- 1–2 married Christians who remember the kinds of questions that mattered before marriage;
- 1–2 mature believers, mentors, pastors, or church-context reviewers who can identify spiritual-safety and pastoral-boundary risks;
- optional: 1 native Ukrainian speaker and 1 native Russian speaker if localized copy is being tested.

Trust requirement:

- participants should understand that this is an early private test, not a public product;
- participants should be comfortable giving honest criticism;
- participants should not be asked to share highly sensitive relationship details;
- no one should be pressured by church leadership, family, or a potential partner to participate.

## 4. Consent, privacy, and staged disclosure

Before testing, tell each participant:

```text
This is a private early test. You may stop at any time. Please do not share anything you would not want included in product feedback notes. We are testing clarity and usefulness of the questions and report, not judging you, your relationship, your readiness, or your future.

Your feedback will be summarized without your name unless you explicitly agree otherwise. Raw answers should not be shared beyond the test facilitator. If anything feels spiritually weighty, painful, unsafe, or too personal, skip it and bring it to trusted human counsel rather than the test.
```

Data handling:

- collect only the minimum needed for product feedback;
- separate product feedback from raw personal answers when possible;
- do not copy raw sensitive answers into the summary report;
- anonymize participant labels as `P1`, `P2`, etc.;
- ask separately before using any direct quote;
- do not share results with pastors, mentors, family, churches, or potential partners without the participant’s explicit consent;
- delete or redact raw answers after synthesis if they are not needed.

## 5. What each participant tests

Each participant should review one of these artifacts, depending on what exists at test time:

1. **Question-only test** — participant goes through the Start Alone questions and gives feedback before report generation exists.
2. **Report-template test** — participant reads a sample `PersonalPreparationMap` produced from fictional or sanitized answers.
3. **End-to-end private test** — participant answers the questions and receives their own generated preparation map.

Use the lowest-risk option that still answers the current product question. If the generated report has not passed the internal generated-report rubric, do not give it to trusted users except as an explicitly marked draft for reviewer-only feedback.

## 6. Session flow

Recommended time: 30–45 minutes per participant.

### Step 1 — Short orientation, 3 minutes

Facilitator says:

```text
Before We Build is meant to help a Christian person slow down before a serious step: name what is clear, notice what is unclear, and prepare for wise conversation before God. It is not a verdict, not pastoral authority, not a compatibility prediction, and not a promise.
```

Then ask:

- In your own words, what do you think this tool is for?
- What do you think it should not be used for?

### Step 2 — Use or read the artifact, 15–25 minutes

Participant completes the question flow, reads a sample report, or reviews the full output.

Observer notes:

- where the participant pauses;
- where wording feels unclear, cold, too technical, or too strong;
- whether they notice the caveat;
- whether they look for a score, verdict, match, or prediction;
- whether any question feels intrusive, shaming, manipulative, or spiritually unsafe.

### Step 3 — Immediate comprehension check, 5 minutes

Ask without coaching:

- What did this output tell you?
- What did it not tell you?
- What would you do next after reading it?
- Would you treat any part of it as a decision or verdict? If yes, which part?

### Step 4 — Feedback interview, 10–15 minutes

Use the questions in section 8.

### Step 5 — Closing safety reminder, 1 minute

Facilitator says:

```text
Thank you. Please remember: this test does not decide anything about your life, relationship, readiness, or future. If anything important came up, bring it to God with Scripture, prayer, church, wise counsel, and personal responsibility.
```

## 7. Measurement dimensions

Use a simple 1–5 rating only as a product feedback aid. Do not average these into a public claim. Do not call them validation scores.

| Dimension | What to measure | Rating anchor |
|---|---|---|
| Clarity | User understands purpose, sections, and next step. | 1 = confusing / 5 = clear enough to explain back |
| Spiritual safety | User feels the tool stays under Scripture, prayer, church, and counsel. | 1 = unsafe or over-authoritative / 5 = humble and bounded |
| Usefulness | User receives concrete questions or topics for wise conversation. | 1 = vague or unhelpful / 5 = practically useful |
| Caveat understanding | User understands “not verdict, not pastoral authority, not promise.” | 1 = missed or misunderstood / 5 = can repeat it accurately |
| Trust without over-trust | User can use the map without treating it as an oracle. | 1 = likely to over-trust / 5 = appropriately cautious |
| Emotional tone | User experiences warmth without pressure, shame, or manipulation. | 1 = cold/shaming/pressuring / 5 = warm and non-pressuring |
| Missing topics | User can name important absent topics. | 1 = many critical gaps / 5 = no major gaps for this stage |
| Privacy confidence | User understands sharing is controlled by them. | 1 = unclear or worrying / 5 = private-by-default is clear |

Required qualitative note for every rating:

```text
What wording, section, or experience caused this rating?
```

## 8. Feedback questions

### A. Clarity

1. In one sentence, what is Before We Build trying to help you do?
2. Which section or question was easiest to understand?
3. Which section or question was confusing, abstract, too technical, or too long?
4. Did any public wording sound like product jargon, psychology language, or internal system language?
5. What title or phrase would you use for this kind of report?

### B. Spiritual safety

6. Did the tool feel like it stayed under Scripture, prayer, church, wise counsel, and personal responsibility?
7. Did any wording sound like it was giving spiritual authority, telling you God’s will, or judging your spiritual state?
8. Did any question or output feel shaming about loneliness, desire for marriage, family hopes, fear, repentance, or uncertainty?
9. Are there topics you think should require review by a mature believer, pastor, or church-context reviewer before public use?
10. What sentence would you add or change to make the boundaries safer?

### C. Usefulness for wise conversation

11. Did the questions help you name anything that would be useful to discuss before a serious step?
12. Did the report give concrete topics, or did it stay too general?
13. Which future conversation question would you actually keep?
14. Which suggested question would you remove or rewrite?
15. After using this, what is one humble next step a person might take?

### D. Caveat understanding

16. What does “not a verdict” mean to you after reading this?
17. What does “not pastoral authority” mean here?
18. What does “not a promise” mean here?
19. Was the caveat placed early enough and written plainly enough?
20. Did any later wording weaken or contradict the caveat?

### E. Trust without over-trust

21. Did any part make you want a score, match label, prediction, or yes/no answer?
22. Did any part sound more certain than it should?
23. Did you feel the tool knew something about your motives or future that you did not directly say?
24. Would a lonely, anxious, or vulnerable user be tempted to over-trust this? Where?
25. What wording would reduce over-trust without making the tool useless?

### F. Privacy and consent

26. Was it clear that sharing is private by default and controlled by the user?
27. Would you feel safe exporting this to a mentor, pastor, or trusted believer if you chose to?
28. What should never be included in an export by default?
29. Did any question feel too personal for an early test?
30. What privacy wording would make you more confident?

### G. Missing topics

31. What important topic before a serious relationship decision was missing?
32. Were faith, church life, family, money, conflict, service, children, boundaries, counsel, and daily responsibilities covered enough for a first version?
33. Did the tool give enough room for “I do not know yet”?
34. What should be asked later, but not in the first session?
35. What should be removed because it is too heavy, too early, or too easy to misuse?

### H. Final judgment for this stage

36. Would you recommend testing this with a few more trusted users after revisions? Why or why not?
37. What is the most important change before public use?
38. What is one thing that should not be changed?
39. If you had to describe this to another Christian in plain language, what would you say?
40. Did this help prepare for wiser conversation, or did it feel like another test to pass?

### 10.2 Claim rules

Allowed claims after 5–10 trusted-user tests:

- “In a small trusted-user test, several participants found [specific section] clear/useful.”
- “Participants misunderstood [specific wording], so it should be revised.”
- “A few participants were tempted to read [phrase] as a verdict, so it should be softened or removed.”
- “This artifact is ready / not ready for another small test round after specific revisions.”

Forbidden claims:

- “Users validated the product.”
- “The tool works.”
- “The tool improves relationship decisions.”
- “The tool predicts compatibility.”
- “The report is spiritually safe for public use.”
- “Christians trust this tool.”
- “This is pastor-approved,” unless an actual named review process with consent says so.

Preferred wording:

```text
This small round gives early design feedback only. It helps identify confusing wording, spiritual-safety risks, useful conversation topics, caveat understanding, over-trust risk, privacy concerns, and missing topics before wider testing.
```

## 11. Pass / revise / stop criteria for this test round

### Continue to another trusted-user round if:

- most participants can explain the purpose without mentioning prediction or verdict;
- caveat meaning is understood by most participants;
- no hard spiritual-safety blocker appears;
- at least several concrete conversation topics are seen as useful;
- privacy and user-controlled sharing are understood;
- revisions are small enough to make before the next round.

### Revise before further testing if:

- participants repeatedly misunderstand the purpose;
- participants ask for or assume scores, match labels, readiness judgments, or predictions;
- caveat wording is skipped or forgotten;
- useful topics are too vague;
- localized wording sounds unnatural or mixed with English/internal terms;
- participants find questions too intrusive, shaming, or pressure-shaped.

### Stop and review before more testing if:

- any wording claims or implies spiritual authority;
- the tool gives or appears to give a relationship verdict;
- it pressures disclosure, pursuit, commitment, reconciliation, or church involvement;
- it mishandles abuse, coercion, self-harm, serious danger, or spiritual manipulation;
- trusted reviewers say the test would be pastorally unsafe without redesign.

## 12. Implementation notes

- Run this only after generated samples pass `docs/generated-report-review-rubric.md` or limit testing to question-only feedback.
- Do not recruit strangers for this first round.
- Do not use advertising, public church announcements, or social-media recruitment yet.
- Prefer individual sessions over group review for privacy.
- Keep the first round small and slow; the goal is to find wording and safety problems early.
- If localized Ukrainian or Russian copy is tested, ask native speakers to mark unnatural phrases and anglicisms directly.
- Treat pastor/mentor feedback as design and safety review, not as delegated spiritual authority for users’ decisions.
